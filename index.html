🧠 Physics & Thermodynamics Knowledge Center

This engine simulates how kinetic energy converts into heat energy when slowing down a high-performance vehicle. Here are the core math principles calculated under the hood:

1. Kinetic Energy & Rotational Overhead

Standard vehicle formulas only look at linear movement mass. Racing simulations scale total system energy using a rotational inertia correction factor (k) to account for spinning wheels, hubs, and un-sprung rotating components:

Total Energy (Joules) = 0.5 * Mass * Velocity² * k

2. Dynamic Braking Performance Limits

Maximum deceleration is physically limited by the friction coefficient between your tires and the asphalt (μ), utilizing standard gravitational pull variables:

Deceleration (m/s²) = Tire Friction (μ) * 9.80665 m/s² Stopping Time (seconds) = Initial Velocity / Deceleration

3. Peak Heat Spikes vs. Soak Temperature

Heat doesn't spread instantly through a solid steel rotor. This simulation runs two models simultaneously:

  • Bulk Mass Overlap (Soak ΔT): Uses the full physical mass of the rotor component to find stable system soaking.
  • Action Surface Path (Track ΔT Max): Restricts thermal distribution to the narrow ring swept directly by the brake pads, highlighting localized extreme thermal fatigue spots.
Temperature Delta (ΔT) = Energy Share / (Material Mass * Specific Heat)
