Brake System Physics Document 📚

1. Fluid Dynamics: Pascal's Law

Hydraulic brakes rely on the principle that pressure applied to a confined liquid is transmitted equally everywhere. The pressure created at the brake pedal is:

Pressure = Pedal Force / Master Cylinder Area

Because the slave cylinder inside the wheel assembly has a much larger surface area, it multiplies your physical foot force:

Clamping Force = Pressure × Slave Cylinder Area

2. Thermodynamics: Energy Conservation

Braking is the process of converting Kinetic Energy into Thermal Energy (Heat) through friction. The heat generated per millisecond in this simulation is calculated as:

Heat Generated = Friction Force × Speed × Time Increment

At the same time, the rotor experiences thermal dissipation, shedding heat back into the environment depending on your chosen cooling variable.

3. Materials Science: Brake Fade

When kinetic energy is converted to heat too rapidly, the brake pads can surpass 250°C. At this point, resin elements inside the pads begin to vaporize, creating a layer of gas that prevents smooth contact with the rotor. This drops your friction coefficient dramatically, requiring significantly more input force to stop.

4. Tire Dynamics: ABS and Grip Limits

The maximum force the tires can exert onto the road surface before breaking traction is limited by mass, gravity, and road conditions:

Max Grip Force = Vehicle Mass × Gravity (9.81) × Road Friction Coefficient (μ)

If your brakes clamp harder than this threshold without ABS, the wheels lock up instantly, causing a SKID. When ABS is enabled, the onboard computer modulates brake pressure right at the threshold limit, preventing lockup and maintaining steering traction.
