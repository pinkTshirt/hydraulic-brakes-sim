🧠 Hydraulics Knowledge Center

Press and hold the pedal above. Every stage of the circuit — pedal leverage, hydraulic amplification, the proportioning valve, and final clamp force — is being calculated live from the inputs in the control panel. Here's the chain of physics behind it:

1. Pedal Leverage

The pedal is a lever. Your foot applies a force at the pad, but the pivot geometry multiplies it before it ever reaches the master cylinder pushrod (pedal ratio, typically 4:1 to 6:1 in road and race applications):

Pushrod Force (N) = Pedal Force (N) * Pedal Ratio

2. Pascal's Law & Hydraulic Pressure

The pushrod compresses the fluid inside the master cylinder's bore. Pressure is force spread over area — and because brake fluid is (essentially) incompressible, that same pressure is transmitted equally to every line and every caliper piston connected to the circuit:

Pressure (Pa) = Pushrod Force (N) / Bore Area (m²), where Bore Area = π * (bore diameter / 2)²

3. Why the Master Cylinder Is Split in Two

Modern brake systems use a tandem (dual-circuit) master cylinder — two pistons in series, each feeding an independent circuit. If one circuit springs a leak, the other piston still builds pressure and you keep partial braking instead of total failure. This diagram routes the secondary (front) piston straight to the front calipers, and the primary (rear) piston through a proportioning valve to the rears. Many road cars instead split the circuits diagonally (one front + the opposite rear per circuit) for more even braking after a single-circuit failure — same safety principle, different plumbing.

4. The Proportioning Valve

Under hard braking, weight transfers forward onto the front tires and off the rears, so the rear tires can supply far less grip than the fronts. Without intervention, equal hydraulic pressure to all four corners would lock the rears first. The proportioning valve lets rear pressure climb 1:1 with the front up to a knee point, then mechanically restricts further rear pressure rise:

Rear Line Pressure = MC Pressure, if MC Pressure ≤ Knee Rear Line Pressure = Knee + (MC Pressure − Knee) * Slope, if MC Pressure > Knee

5. Caliper Clamp Force & Brake Torque

At the wheel, line pressure pushes directly on the caliper piston face(s), squeezing the pad against the rotor. That clamp force, acting at the rotor's effective friction radius on both pad faces, is what actually generates stopping torque — the same torque value that feeds the kinetic-energy and thermal model on the main simulation page:

Clamp Force (N) = Line Pressure (Pa) * Piston Area (m²) * Piston Count Brake Torque (N·m) = 2 * Pad μ * Clamp Force (N) * Effective Rotor Radius (m)

Note the "Force Gain" stat above the diagram: a few hundred newtons on the pedal routinely turns into multiple kilonewtons of clamp force at each caliper. That multiplication is the combined product of pedal leverage and the bore/piston area ratio — Pascal's Law doing the heavy lifting.
